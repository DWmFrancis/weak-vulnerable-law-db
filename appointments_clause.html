Appointments Clause – U.S. Constitution, Article II, §2, Clause 2

Watchlist
Risk Score:
3.9 / 5
Vulnerability Types:
  • Norm Dependency
  • Ambiguity
  • Bypass Loopholes
Summary:

The Appointments Clause governs how federal officers are appointed, typically requiring Senate confirmation. However, the use of “acting” appointments and strategic vacancies has allowed Presidents to circumvent the intended checks. Courts have struggled to clearly define limits, leaving the door open for manipulation of federal staffing.

Historical Use:
  • Extended use of acting officials without Senate confirmation (e.g., DHS and DOJ leadership)
  • Vacancy Act used to justify multiple temporary appointments
Flagged Scenarios:
  • Filling key roles with loyalists without confirmation hearings
  • Leaving roles officially vacant to avoid oversight
  • Deploying acting heads to advance controversial policies rapidly
Suggested Reforms:
  • Limit the time acting officials can serve without Senate approval
  • Mandate public disclosure of all acting appointments and expiration dates
  • Clarify legal authority boundaries of non-confirmed officials
